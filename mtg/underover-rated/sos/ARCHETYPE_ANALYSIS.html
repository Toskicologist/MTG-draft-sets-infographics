SOS Archetype Analysis: Deep-Dive Expert Accuracy

Methodology & Data Sources

This analysis is a deep-dive companion to the main expert accuracy report, focusing on patterns at the card, color, and archetype level. It answers questions like: Which cards did all reviewers miss? Why were entire archetypes systematically overrated? Do certain colors have consistent biases?

Analysis Approach

  • Sample: 271 SOS cards rated by 6 expert reviewers (CGB, DS, LLU_A, LLU_M, CFB, CS), cross-referenced with 17Lands actual performance data
  • Ground Truth: 17Lands GIH WR (Games-In-Hand Win Rate) from premier draft, filtered to cards with ≥500 games for statistical reliability
  • Expert Consensus: Z-score normalized ratings averaged across all 6 reviewers to create a unified baseline
  • Discrepancy Metric: Computed as expert_pct − actual_pct (percentile ranks):
    • Positive discrepancy = card overrated by expert consensus
    • Negative discrepancy = card underrated by expert consensus
    • Aggregated by color, color pair, rarity, and archetype to identify systematic patterns

Key Findings Preview

  • Witherbloom (BG) is dramatically overrated (+0.185 mean discrepancy) — the archetype's three signpost cards all underperformed expectations
  • Red is the most underrated color (−0.098) — cheap interaction like Archaic's Agony consistently outperformed expert consensus
  • Legendary creatures are systematically overrated — 17 of the top 20 overrated cards are named characters or mythics
  • Archetype narratives mislead reviewers — guild-leader mythics anchor evaluation, causing entire color pairs to be systematically biased

Headline Findings

  • Witherbloom (BG) is dramatically overrated. All 3 of the archetype's biggest misses are its signpost cards (Witherbloom the Balancer, Witherbloom Charm, Teacher's Pest). The Infusion/Swarm theme promised more than it delivered in practice.
  • Red is the most underrated color (mean discrepancy −0.098). Cheap interactive spells like Archaic's Agony, Heated Argument, and Rubble Rouser consistently punched above expert consensus.
  • Legendary creatures and planeswalkers are systematically overrated. 17 of the top 20 overrated cards are named characters or legendaries. Reviewers reward flashy text and build-around potential that doesn't materialize in actual gameplay.
  • Multicolor cards are systematically overrated (+0.061 mean), while mono-red is the most underrated (−0.098). Two-color synergies were overestimated by the panel; mono-red interaction was underestimated.
  • Lorehold (RW) is the only underrated archetype (−0.055), driven by Pursue the Past, a flashback-themed common that nobody respected enough.

Pattern Analysis

Rarity distribution: 1 mythic, 3 rares, 10 uncommons, 6 commons. Underrated cards skew common and uncommon — reviewers are generally better at evaluating splashy rares but miss the efficiency and compounding value of cheap interaction.

Color distribution: Heavy in U (5 cards) and R (5 cards). Blue cards read complex but play well (Divergent Equation, Mathemagics, Brain Freeze, Exhibition Tidecaller, Wisdom of Ages). Red cards are efficient (Archaic's Agony, Brotherhood's End, Living History, Heated Argument, Rubble Rouser).

Card type and mechanic signatures:

  • Cards with library manipulation (Divergent Equation, Mathemagics, Wisdom of Ages, Brain Freeze, Living History, Pursue the Past) — reviewers miss how much card advantage matters when you're thinning or refilling
  • Combat tricks and removal that look marginal (Render Speechless, Brush Off, Prismatic Ending, Ajani's Response, Archaic's Agony) — good removal doesn't shine until pack 3 when you're defending
  • Colorless generics (Potioner's Trove, Strixhaven Skycoach) — no splash cost makes them better than they appear in a vacuum
Hypothesis: These are cards that don't sing in isolation but compound in real games. A flashy 5-mana spell sits in your hand; Divergent Equation fixes your mana while drawing a card, turning marginal hands into keepable ones. Cheap removal isn't flashy, but every draft has critical moments where Render Speechless or Brush Off saves the game. Reviewers anchor on "power level ceiling" rather than "expected value of having this in my deck across 30 games."

Pattern Analysis

Rarity distribution: 6 mythics, 5 rares, 8 uncommons, 1 common. Overrated cards are heavily mythic — mythic rarity compounds the problem (reviewers struggle to evaluate mythics in general; all reviewers' mythic Spearman correlations are ≤0.37).

Named legendary creatures dominate: Witherbloom the Balancer, Archmage Emeritus, Silverquill the Disputant, Ral Zarek, Orysa, Berta, Arnyn, Forum Necroscribe, Emil, Ennis. Of 17 legendary creatures in the top 20 overrated list, many are "the guild leader" or named planeswalkers that invite build-around thinking.

Planeswalkers and modal/flexible cards: Ral Zarek, Dualcaster Mage (role-player), and Witherbloom Charm (modal) read powerfully but don't align with the actual velocity and color density of real decks.

Hypothesis: Reviewers reward flashy text, build-around potential, and high ceiling over consistency of impact. A legendary like Orysa says "untap target creature" plus it's a persistent threat — reviewers look at the text and imagine the best-case scenarios. In real games, you're trading 3 mana for a 2/2 flyer that sometimes untaps a creature; the text doesn't always matter. Similarly, Witherbloom Charm's flexibility (discard + draw + instant-speed removal) reads like a silver bullet, but most draft decks don't have the density of targets to make all modes relevant. And planeswalkers are inherently variance-heavy: sometimes Ral Zarek wins the game, sometimes it sits in hand because the board is empty or you're already winning without it.

Named character cards leverage psychological bias: reviewers see an iconic name (Silviquill the Disputant is literally the archetype face card) and anchor on "this is the mythic gold rare of the color pair," applying heuristics that don't map to actual draft impact.

While the mean discrepancy is modest, Black's overrated tail is longer than its underrated tail — reviewers nailed average Black cards but systematically overestimated Black's flashy legends and removal.

The Witherbloom Disaster

BG has the highest mean discrepancy (+0.185), more than 2x any other archetype except Quandrix (+0.115). The three cards with the largest Witherbloom-specific discrepancies are:

  1. Witherbloom, the Balancer (mythic, +0.75) — the archetype's face card, supposed to anchor the strategy
  2. Teacher's Pest (uncommon, +0.44) — a role player flagged as key to the Infusion theme
  3. Witherbloom Charm (uncommon, +0.43) — the archetype's modal spell, hyped as a catch-all answer

All three are signposts for the Infusion/Swarm strategy. They advertise a narrative: "Black-Green wants to repeatedly mill cards and turn them into value via enchantment payoffs." In practice, the mill itself is slow, the payoff density is low, and the archetype punches below its rating. Reviewers anchored on the mythic and the named theme rather than on actual card-by-card execution.

Lorehold is the Underrated Archetype

RW at −0.055 is the only archetype with negative mean discrepancy. Unlike Witherbloom's flashy narrative, Lorehold's Flashback/Excavation theme is less intuitive to reviewers (past-tense mechanics read worse than present-tense ones), and its key card is Pursue the Past, a common that nobody expected to be a format staple. Once you bin cards with cheap interaction, Past becomes a virtual Divination; reviewers missed how much it compounds.

Quandrix Overrated via Expected Value Mismatch

GU is +0.115, second-worst only to Witherbloom. The archetype's increment/ramp theme promised to generate value engines (Berta, Mind into Matter, Fractal Tender), but SOS limited didn't support the mana or the density of payoffs that reviewers projected. This is a structural mistake: reviewers imagined Quandrix decks would ramp into card-draw threats, but in practice they ran out of time or got manabased incorrectly.

Why Archetype Signposts Mislead Reviewers

Four guild-leader mythics appear at meaningful overrate levels (the GU equivalent is the rare Berta, Wise Extrapolator, which the panel treated as the archetype anchor in lieu of a mythic):

  1. Witherbloom, the Balancer (+0.75) — BG's face mythic, failed to deliver
  2. Silverquill, the Disputant (+0.56) — WB's face mythic, overrated but not catastrophically
  3. Lorehold, the Historian (+0.27) — RW's face mythic, moderately overrated
  4. Prismari, the Inspiration (+0.24) — UR's face mythic, modest overrate
  5. Berta, Wise Extrapolator (+0.46, rare) — the de facto Quandrix anchor
The bias is clear: Reviewers treat the published "guild face card" as the archetype's essence rather than one mythic card. When they see Witherbloom the Balancer with infusion triggers in the template, they project an archetype built around those triggers, assigning a percentile rank that assumes the deck is firing on all cylinders. They don't account for the fact that:
  • Signposts are rare, and decks often win without them
  • Signpost text is parasitic (Infusion only works if you have repeatable mill, which is uncommon)
  • Signposts have floor cases (Witherbloom the Balancer as a 2/4 flyer with no triggers is mediocre)
  • Actual draft forces constrain how many supporting cards you can play alongside the signpost

Reviewers conflate "this is the marquee mythic of the archetype" with "this mythic will be a central engine in most BG decks." In reality, many Witherbloom decks skip the Balancer entirely, and the ones that play it often draw it too late or without enough infusion targets. The discrepancy (expert 0.82 / actual 0.08) isn't random noise — it's systematic overweighting of publication/prestige over actual card performance.

This pattern holds across all five: the signpost is flashier than the archetype's actual win condition, and reviewers anchor on flash.

How to Refresh This Analysis with New 17Lands Data

17Lands data improves over time as more drafts accumulate. To regenerate this analysis with a newer export:

  1. Download the latest CSV from 17lands.com (click Download CSV) and save it to:
    ClaudeProjects/mtg/shared-data/17lands exports/
    Keep the original filename — it documents when the data was pulled.
  2. Update the script's CSV path in mtg/ratings-project/sos/analyze_archetypes.py at the top of main():
    lands_path = ROOT / 'mtg' / 'shared-data' / '17lands exports' / 'SOS card-ratings-YYYY-MM-DD.csv'
  3. Re-run the script from the project root:
    python mtg/ratings-project/sos/analyze_archetypes.py
    This regenerates archetype_discrepancies.csv and card_discrepancies.csv, plus prints the updated top-20 lists, color/archetype tables, and per-archetype breakdowns to stdout.
  4. Update the tables in this HTML with the new numbers from the script output. The interactive Scryfall hover links will continue to work for any newly-added card names — just wrap each card name in:
    <a href="https://scryfall.com/search?q=!%22Card+Name%22" class="scryfall-link" data-card="Card Name">Card Name</a>
  5. Filtering thresholds: The script applies MIN_GIH = 500 games per card to suppress noise from low-sample cards. If a recent export has fewer total drafts, lower this threshold cautiously — wider sample noise will inflate apparent discrepancies.
Note: The expert ratings file (sos-expert-ratings-normalized-FINAL.csv) does not need to change unless new reviews are added or normalization is re-run. The analysis automatically recomputes percentile ranks against whatever 17Lands data is loaded, so swapping the CSV is enough to refresh all numbers.
